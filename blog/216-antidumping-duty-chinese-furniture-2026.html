U.S. Imposes 216% Antidumping Duty on Chinese Bedroom Furniture — Key Facts & Actions

By Bonnie Woo | April 22, 2026 | 5 min read

⚠️ Key Alert for Importers

On April 13, 2026, the U.S. Department of Commerce ruled that 11 Chinese wooden bedroom furniture companies failed to qualify for a separate antidumping rate. They will now face the full 216.01% China-wide cash deposit rate.

As someone who has spent over 20 years in international trade and B2B sourcing, I have seen how quickly regulatory changes can reshape supply chains. This ruling is one of the most significant developments in recent years for anyone sourcing furniture or home decor products from China. Here is what you need to know.

What Happened?

On April 13, 2026, the U.S. Department of Commerce issued a preliminary determination that 11 Chinese wooden bedroom furniture exporters did not qualify for separate, company-specific antidumping duty rates. As a result, these companies were automatically assigned to the "China-wide entity" and are now subject to the maximum cash deposit rate of 216.01%.

This means that for every $100 of furniture value, importers must deposit $216.01 with U.S. Customs at the time of entry — effectively making these products uncompetitive in the U.S. market.

Why Does This Matter?

Under U.S. trade law, China is classified as a non-market economy. In practical terms, this means:

  • All Chinese exporters are presumed to be government-controlled unless they prove otherwise.
  • Companies must apply for a separate rate during each annual administrative review.
  • This separate rate is typically significantly lower than the China-wide rate.
  • Companies that do not apply — or fail to meet the evidentiary burden — are automatically grouped into the China-wide entity.

The separate rate application process is not optional. It is a mandatory, annual compliance requirement for any Chinese exporter wanting to maintain market access in the United States.

What Went Wrong for the 11 Companies?

10 Companies: No Application Filed

The majority of the affected companies — 10 out of 11 — simply did not submit a separate-rate application during the review period. Under Commerce Department rules, no filing equals automatic inclusion in the China-wide entity. There is no grace period and no appeals process for this particular failure.

Shenzhen Xinfudu Furniture: Inconsistent Declaration

The 11th company, Shenzhen Xinfudu Furniture, claimed it had made no shipments to the U.S. during the 2024 review period. However, U.S. Customs import data contradicted this claim, showing shipments had in fact occurred. Combined with the failure to file a separate-rate application, this inconsistency sealed the same outcome.

📌 Lesson for Suppliers

Accuracy in export declarations is not just a compliance issue — it directly affects your duty rate and market access. Ensure your export records match U.S. Customs data precisely.

What Buyers Should Do Now

✅ Immediate Actions for Importers

  • Verify supplier status before placing orders. Ask for their most recent separate-rate determination letter from the Commerce Department.
  • Update purchase contracts to allocate tariff and duty risk clearly. Specify which party bears the cost if rates change.
  • Diversify sourcing to reduce exposure. Consider alternative manufacturing locations such as Vietnam, Malaysia, and Indonesia.
  • Audit your current supplier base. If any of your suppliers are among the 11 affected companies, you need a contingency plan immediately.

What Suppliers Should Do Now

✅ Compliance Checklist for Exporters

  • Apply every year during the annual administrative review. Mark your calendar — missing the deadline is not an option.
  • Respond fully and promptly to all Commerce Department questionnaires. Partial responses are treated as non-responses.
  • Keep accurate records and ensure your export data matches U.S. Customs import data exactly.
  • If no shipments, formally declare "no shipments" with supporting evidence — do not simply ignore the review.
  • Hire experienced trade counsel. Antidumping law is complex; expert guidance is a necessary investment, not an optional expense.

What Is Next?

The April 13 ruling is preliminary. The Commerce Department is expected to issue its final determination in August 2026. The final rate could still change, but the trend is clear: Commerce is tightening scrutiny on separate-rate eligibility, and the evidentiary burden on Chinese exporters is increasing.

Importers should monitor the Federal Register for updates and factor potential rate changes into their 2026-2027 sourcing plans.

Bottom Line

Tariffs grab headlines, but a 216% antidumping duty eliminates price competitiveness entirely. This is not a cost that can be absorbed or passed on — it changes the fundamental economics of the transaction.

For buyers, knowing your supplier's compliance status is no longer a "nice to have" — it is essential due diligence. For suppliers, investing in compliance infrastructure before losing market access is the only viable path forward.
